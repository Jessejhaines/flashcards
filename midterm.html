What is the building envelope?

The building envelope is the physical barrier that separates the interior and exterior of a building. It includes walls, roofs, floors, windows, and doors, and serves to protect the interior from the elements while contributing to energy efficiency.

What forces act on a building?

Various forces act on a building, including wind, gravity, thermal forces, moisture, seismic forces, and thermal expansion. These forces can impact the buildingâ€™s structural integrity and durability.

What is kinetic moisture flow?

Kinetic moisture flow refers to the movement of water vapor from areas of higher vapor pressure to areas of lower vapor pressure.

What is gravity moisture flow?

Gravity moisture flow is the movement of liquid water driven by the force of gravity, typically moving downward through materials.

What is surface tension?

Surface tension is a property of water that causes it to "stick" to surfaces and resist separation.

What is capillary action?

Capillary action is the movement of water within small pores or spaces, such as within porous materials, moving against the force of gravity.

What is storage capacity?

Storage capacity refers to the ability of a material to absorb and hold moisture.

What is advective moisture flow?

Advective moisture flow occurs when moisture is transferred by the movement of air.

What is convective moisture flow?

Convective moisture flow refers to the transfer of moisture through the movement of air masses.

What is the dew point?

The dew point is the temperature at which air becomes saturated with moisture and water vapor condenses into liquid.

What is conduction?

Conduction is the transfer of heat through a material by direct contact, such as heat moving through walls or windows.

What is convection?

Convection is the transfer of heat through the movement of fluids, like air or water.

What is radiation?

Radiation is the transfer of heat through electromagnetic waves, such as heat from the sun.

What is thermal resistance?

Thermal resistance (RSI) measures how well a material resists heat flow, while thermal transmittance (USI or U-value) measures the amount of heat passing through a material.

What are air films?

Air films are thin layers of air near a surface that resist heat flow, helping to improve insulation.

What is the stack effect?

The stack effect is the upward movement of warm air inside a building, leading to air infiltration at lower levels.

What is thermal bridging?

Thermal bridging occurs when heat flows through areas of a building envelope where thermal resistance is low, often due to continuous metal or other materials.

What is melting vs freezing?

Melting is the transition from solid to liquid, while freezing is the reverse, from liquid to solid.

What is condensation vs evaporation?

Condensation is when water vapor turns into liquid, while evaporation is liquid turning into vapor.

What is deposition vs sublimation?

Deposition is the transition from gas to solid, while sublimation is from solid to gas.

What is a drainage plane in a cavity wall?

A drainage plane allows water to flow away from the wall assembly, preventing moisture buildup.

What is the difference between wet and dry zones in a wall?

Wet zones accumulate water, while dry zones are less likely to have water exposure.

What is thermal control in building construction?

Thermal control refers to managing the flow of heat to maintain a comfortable indoor temperature.

What is vapour control in building construction?

Vapour control manages the movement of moisture in vapor form, preventing condensation inside building materials.

What is air control in building construction?

Air control prevents the unwanted movement of air, which can carry moisture or heat into or out of the building.

What is water control in building construction?

Water control manages the movement of liquid water to prevent moisture damage to building components.

What is water shedding in building construction?

Water shedding refers to the design or techniques used to direct water away from vulnerable parts of the building envelope to prevent water penetration.

What are mass walls?

Mass walls are thick walls made of heavy materials (like concrete or brick) that absorb and release moisture slowly.

What are barrier / face-seal walls?

Barrier or face-seal walls rely on a continuous waterproof barrier to prevent water from entering the interior.

What are cavity / rainscreen walls?

Cavity or rainscreen walls consist of an outer layer that sheds water, a gap for airflow, and an inner layer that prevents water penetration.
